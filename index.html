Shih-chan Dai

Political Scientist

Bio

I am currently a postdoctoral researcher at the UCLA Asia Pacific Center. I received my Ph.D. degree of Political Science at the University of Massachusetts Amherst. My research is situated at the intersection of political communication, LGBTQ politics in East Asia, and social movements.

My work focuses mainly on answering questions about how perceptions of minority groups are constructed. The dissertation project "Why do Policy Frames Change? Rhetorical Construction and Contestation of Gay Rights in a Contested Regime" examines the temporal variation of policy frames regarding gay rights in Taiwan. I look closely at how the three factors (opportunity structure, audience responses, and social positions of actors) lead to changes of frames over time. My works have been published in the Journal of Electoral Studies, Women's Studies International Forum, and Sustainability.

Teaching

Introduction to Comparative Politics

This course explores the major debates in comparative politics regarding the following topics: states, economic systems, democratization, political regimes, inequality, and resistance. This course is designed to make students aware of the political processes that happen outside the context of the United States. Students will learn the essential analytical frameworks to better understand the factors that shape a wide range of political outcomes in a comparative context.

Political Communication

This course is designed to introduce the essential concepts and ideas related to how the communication of information shapes our political lives. In the current digital age, the production, construction, and distribution of information have become even more influential in how politics works. Therefore, there is an increasing interest in unpacking the mechanisms of political communication. This course begins with familiarizing students with some of the fundamental concepts such as “power”, “mediatization”, “ideology”, “intentionality”, and “media channels.” I will also discuss the major theoretical and analytical frameworks in the following realms of political communication: media effects, politics-media relationships, theories of new media,and normative perspectives. The second part of this course highlights discussions on the different channels and actors of political communication. We focus on the roles played by political elites, the masses, influencers of social media, as well as activists and try to understand their behavioral patterns. Therefore, the second part of the course provides a detailed overview of these important topics: political elites’ electoral advertisements and negative campaigning; social movements and framing; interpersonal political communication and cross-cutting networks; social media and the spread of misinformation; mass media and political polarization.The third part of this course looks at how we can apply what we have learned in the first two parts of the class in real-world cases. These case studies are saliently special topics in the research of political communication of recent years. These cases include but are not limited to the following ones: media censorship in China, policy framing of gay rights in Taiwan, former U.S. president Trump’s use of Twitter, and political campaigns of female politicians in Japan.

Social Movements and Collective Action

This course provides an overview of the theories and concepts in the literature of social movement and collective action. We will first review the theoretical perspectives and examine the factors that lead to the emergence and diffusion of political resistance. We will then situate social movements and activists in a web of relations and discuss how they interact with elites, mass public, countermovements, technology, and political institutions. The last part of this course will examine recent cases of movements like the BLM movement, Hong Kong protests in 2014 and 2019.

Quantitative Methods (I)

This course consists of three major themes: basic statistical tools and probability theory (Week1-4), hypothesis testing (Week 5–9), and regression analysis (Week 10 –13). These themes serve as the foundation for students to pursue further studies in advanced statistical analysis and methods such as models with fixed and random effects, time series data, and Bayesian modeling. The order of the themes is arranged in a way to ensure that students understand the fundamental (probability, descriptive statistics, probability distribution, and sampling distribution) before knowing how to make statistical inference (hypothesis tests and regression analysis). In addition to the lectures, we have labs in which the students will learn how to use R codes to carry out analytical tasks related to the topics we have covered in class.

Data Analysis in Social Sciences

This course introduces to students some fundamental concepts and techniques of statistics and quantitative methods used in social sciences. Students will learn basic knowledge of probability and computational skills for data management and analysis. It covers probability, descriptive statistics, probability distributions, hypothesis testing and linear regression models. Students will become familiarized with the R program, R studio, and important R packages such as dplyr, ggplot2, and tidyr.

Framing Analysis

In this course, students will examine one of the core concepts in political communication, framing. We will review theoretical discussions on how various actors (political elites, mass media, activists, and the mass public) construct, perceive and communicate the political reality. The course discusses (1) the methodological approaches to analyze frames, (2) impacts of political communication and frames, (3) case study on the relationship between framing and a variety of political issues like women's rights, populism, and immigration.
